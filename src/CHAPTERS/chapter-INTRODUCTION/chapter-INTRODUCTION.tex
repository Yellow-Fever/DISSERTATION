\section{Historical Significance of the Virus}
\Gls{yfv} is the causative agent of \gls{yf} disease in humans, which occupies a singular and fearsome place in the study and management of tropical illness. \Gls{yf} is noted in colonial-era literature as a febrile disease of the tropics, from which patients would suffer fever, and jaundice, eventually succumbing to hemorrhagic disease \parencite{Vainio:1998wy}. Disease occurrence was recorded in the late 1700s through early 1900s, in which the disease phenotype and eventually the complex mosquito-primate transmission cycle became described. Notably, losses to the disease stemming from American military deployments to Cuba during the Spanish-American War and construction of the Panama Canal prompted close study of the disease in the late nineteenth century \parencite{Bazin:2011vi}. \Gls{yf} occurred along the North American east coast and Gulf of Mexico until 1905, when the last outbreak event of the United States mainland was recorded New Orleans, Louisiana. Transatlantic trade in both goods and persons are considered to have seeded, and eventually permitted the permanent establishment of the virus in South American circulation from West Africa \parencite{Bryant:2007kx}.

\section{YFV is the Prototype Flavivirus}
\Gls{yfv} is the causative agent of \gls{yf}. The virus is a member of the family \emph{Flaviviridae}, genus \emph{flavivirus}, and as such bears similarities of size and genome organization to the related \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{denv}}\gls{denv}, \gls{jev}, and \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{wnv}}\gls{wnv}, and others of public health significance. The particle is enveloped, measuring between 50 and 60nm by transmission electron microscopy \parencite{BurlaudGaillard:2014ia}. The \gls{yfv} genome is organized as a single-stranded, positive-sense strand of \gls{rna}, comprising in read order, a 5’ \gls{utr} (118 bases), three structural proteins \gls{c}, \gls{prm}, \gls{e}, 7 \gls{ns} proteins \gls{ns}1, \gls{ns}2A, \gls{ns}3, \gls{ns}4A, \gls{ns}4B, \gls{ns}5, and a 3’\gls{utr} (507 bases) [Figure \ref{YFV_genome}]. Following clathrin-dependent endocytosis and subsequent low pH endosomal fusion, the genome is immediately translated upon entry to the host cytoplasm, producing a single 3411 amino acid polyprotein that is co- and post-translationally cleaved by a combination of host and viral proteases. Nonstructural genome products contribute to the replication complex and serve various roles in genome replication, which occurs on endoplasmic reticular membrane structures; the assembled virus is transited, and finally exocytosed through the trans-golgi network. 

\section{Replication Cycle and Molecular Biology of YFV}
Owing to the success of vaccination in the prevention of \gls{yf} disease, the specific molecular biology of \gls{yfv} is poorly understood and infrequently researched; in many cases \gls{yfv} molecular biology is interpreted or reviewed by expectation of homology to other flaviviruses, usually in the context of vaccinology \parencite{Monath:2013cu}. For \gls{yfv}, the pathogenic influences exerted by specific protein components of the virus may indicate specific mechanisms of attenuation for \gls{yfv} vaccines, the central interest of this work. As such, the following subsections describing functional properties of flavivirus proteins are described in reference to several members of the family \emph{Flaviviridae}. The topology of the replication and assembly complex is itself obscure, with considerable research efforts spent on elucidation of both the orientation and interactions of virus components in the complex. Listed biophysical properties of the peptides are calculated from Genbank accession AY640589, the full-length \gls{wt} prototype Asibi strain \parencite{McElroy:2005ik}.

\subsection{Structural Genes}
Three structural genes are arranged sequentially in read order along the canonical flavivirus genome [Figure \ref{YFV_genome}], which in combination confer the physical structure of the mature particle.

\subsubsection{Capsid (C)}
\Gls{c} arises from a peptide of 121 residues, with a molecular weight of 13.6\gls{kda}, and pI of 13.05. Canonically, \gls{c} binds and encapsidates \gls{vrna} in the mature particle \parencite{Monath:2013cu}. The protein is observed as a dimer in solution, and the ablation of domains influencing the dimer arrangement were associated with loss of infectivity for cloned \gls{yfv} \parencite{Patkar:2007ga}. Mature flavivirus \gls{c} (11\gls{kda}) possesses an internal hydrophobic domain that mediates association of the C/\gls{vrna} complex to the viral membrane \parencite{Ma:2004dh}. Flavivirus \gls{c} is also observed to aggregate on intracellular lipid structures and as such is thought to influence early stages of particle assembly \parencite{Samsa:2009bq}. 

\subsubsection{Premembrane (prM) and Membrane (M)}
\Gls{prm} arises from a peptide of 164 residues, with a molecular weight of 18.7\gls{kda} and pI of 10.15. \Gls{prm} is a surface component of the nascent virus particle, of which maturation before assembly requires highly regulated cleavage of \gls{prm} (\gls{er} lumen) and \gls{c} (cytoplasmic) from both sides of an intervening, signal peptide which spans the \gls{er} membrane \parencite{Lobigs:2010em}. Maturation of the nascent virus particle through the trans-golgi complex is mediated in part by furin cleavage of the N-terminal prM peptide from the surface of the virion, with \gls{m} remaining. If cleavage of \gls{prm} is not carried through to completion, infectivity is reduced \parencite{Wengler:1989tc}. An apoptosis-promoting domain was observed in \gls{denv} \gls{m}, which in \gls{yfv} 17D strain vaccines (later discussed) an amino acid substitution at residue 36 is observed \parencite{Catteau:2003dl}.

\subsubsection{Envelope (E)}
\Gls{yfv} \gls{e} arises from a peptide of 493 residues, with a molecular weight of 53.2\gls{kda} and pI of 8.11. Flavivirus \gls{e} canonically forms the bulk of outward-facing surface structure for the virion, and as such the protein exerts pathogenically significant roles in physical interaction with the host cell and immunogenicity of the particle. Three domains have been described for the protein, of which domains I and II are discontinuously arranged proceeding from the amino terminus, domain III is continuously translated balance of 100 amino acids oriented C-terminally to domains I and II, with the terminal 8 amino acids consisting of a stem-loop membrane anchor \parencite{Monath:2013cu}.

Envelope domains II and III occupy, in their folded states, opposite ends of domain I, of which the entire complex is arranged in homodimeric structures along the surface of the virion. Domain II contains both dimerization and fusion properties, e.g. it confers the capacity of the mature \gls{e} complex to dimerize and to fuse with the host endosome, respectively. Following receptor-mediated endocytosis of the particle, endosomal fusion takes place via a pH-dependent mechanism in which the low pH of the endosome provokes a rearrangement of the dimer structure into trimers that present the domain II fusion loop and execute the fusion into the host cytoplasm \parencite{Monath:2013cu}. Domain III is thought to govern interactions with host receptors, however very few studies have demonstrated either a definitive receptor or the particular residues influencing the binding affinity of domain III \parencite{Lee:2008gd}. Both domains II and III are immunogenic and stimulate neutralizing antibody against the particle, however domains I and II are observed to solicit the predominant bulk of neutralizing antibody response \parencite{Vratskikh:2013gk}.

\subsection{Nonstructural Genes}
Seven structural genes are arranged sequentially in read order along the canonical flavivirus genome following the structural genes; they have diverse and sometimes poorly understood roles within the infected host cell.

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 1 (NS1)}
\Gls{ns}1 arises from a peptide of 352 residues, with a molecular weight of 39.7\gls{kda} and pI of 7.98. The protein is found in both intracellular and secreted forms, of which the extracellular form is highly immunogenic. For \gls{yfv}, the \gls{ns}1 is found in membrane-bound \parencite{Schlesinger:1990hh} and secreted forms, which may be glycosylated \parencite{Post:1991vg}. Secreted forms of \gls{ns}1 are observed in a hexameric form, which in \gls{denv} infection of \gls{ifn}-$\alpha$ receptor-knockout mice was found to contribute to vascular leakage, a manifestation of severe disease \parencite{Beatty:2015it}. Intracellular \gls{ns}1 is required for replication competence of the virus and associates with \gls{ns}4B (discussed later), providing evidence for association of the protein with the replication complex \parencite{Youn:2012jt, Youn:2013hj}. 

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 2A (NS2A)}
\Gls{ns}2A arises from a peptide of 224 residues, with a molecular weight of 24.1\gls{kda} and pI of 11.03. The protein is hydrophobic, and likely plays a role in
the replication complex, in assembly, and in membrane induction \parencite{Leung:2008cv}. Destructive mutagenesis of the interaction between \gls{ns}2A and \gls{ns}3 ablates virion assembly \parencite{Vobmann:2015dy}. 

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 2B (NS2B)}
\Gls{ns}2B arises from a peptide of 130 residues, with a molecular weight of 13.8\gls{kda} and pI of 4.40. \gls{ns}2B functions in complex with \gls{ns}3 and is required for protease activity to occur \parencite{Monath:2013cu}.

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 3}
\Gls{ns}3 arises from a peptide of 623 residues, with a molecular weight of 69.013.8\gls{kda} and pI of 8.69. The protein contains three functional domains. First a serine protease domain assists in cleavage of the single virus polypeptide \parencite{Monath:2013cu}. A second nucleotide triphosphatase domain catalyses the removal of phosphate groups from nascent nucleotide triphosphate during replication. Finally, an \gls{rna} helicase is employed to unwind \glspl{rna} during minus strand synthesis. The protein contains immunodominant CD8$^+$ T cell epitopes \parencite{vanderMost:2002eo}. 

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 4A}
\Gls{ns}4A arises from a peptide of 149 residues, with a molecular weight of 16.0\gls{kda} and and pI of 6.10. \Gls{ns}4A associates with \gls{ns}4B, and thus is thought to have a role in the replication complex \parencite{Zou:2015dl}.

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 4B}
\Gls{ns}4B arises from a peptide of 250 residues, with a molecular weight of 27.3\gls{kda} and pI of 9.87. \Gls{ns}4B is very hydrophobic and is localized to \gls{er} membranes during viral replication. The protein is thought to interact with the replication complex and provide stability or orientation as a homodimeric stucture \parencite{Zou:2014ku}. The C-terminus of flavivirus \gls{ns}4B is reported to be an \gls{ifn} antagonist \parencite{MunozJordan:2005ia}.

\subsubsection{Nonstructural Protein 5}
\Gls{ns}5 arises from a peptide of 906 residues, with a molecular weight of 103.4\gls{kda} and and pI of 9.68. \Gls{ns}5 exerts two main functions, which are, respectively, (1) a methyltransferase domain, which participates in 5' capping of the nascent \glspl{vrna}, and (2), the \gls{rna}-dependent \gls{rna} polymerase, which synthesizes \gls{rna} from an \gls{rna} template \parencite{Monath:2013cu}. For \gls{yfv}, \gls{ns}5 contains determinants of neurovirulence in small mammalian models \parencite{Xie:1998wq}. Evidence has been put forth to suggest the involvement of flavivirus \gls{ns}5 in antagonism of innate \gls{ifn} responses in the mammalian host by the blockade of JAK-STAT signaling in \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{lgtv}}\gls{lgtv} infection \parencite{Best:2005kg}. Further, decreased surface expression of the \gls{ifn}-$\alpha$ receptor was observed in \gls{wnv} infection \parencite{Lubick:2015fg}. Specifically for \gls{yfv}, a positive feedback mechanism has been described for \gls{ns}5 in which the propagation of type 1 \gls{ifn} signals causes ubiquitination of \gls{ns}5, permitting its sequestration of STAT2, dampening the transduction of \gls{ifn} responses \parencite{LaurentRolle:2014hn}. 

\subsection{Untranslated Regions}
\subsubsection{5' Untranslated Region(UTR)}
 The 5'\gls{utr} is 119 nucleotides in length (\gls{yfv}), and is highly conserved. The region contains secondary structures attributed to cyclization of the virus genome \emph{in vivo}.
 
\subsubsection{3' Untranslated Region(UTR)}
The 3'\gls{utr} is 508 nucleotides in length, and also contains secondary structures, however there is variability in the sequence and secondary structures among flaviviruses and between \gls{yfv} genotypes. Most interestingly, a set of tandem repeat deletions in the 3'\gls{utr} were associated geographically, meaning that some ecological factor exerts pressure to keep the deletions \parencite{Bryant:2005dq}.


\section{Ecology and Epidemiology}
\Gls{wt} \gls{yfv} is principally vectored by \emph{Aedes spp.} mosquitoes in Africa and \emph{Haemogogus} and \emph{Sabethes spp.} in South America and the non-human primate hosts differ by geographic region. The taxonomy of \gls{yfv} implies considerable ecological and geographic restriction for the virus; sustained transmission has only been observed in tropical and subtropical belts of South America, Africa, and certain Caribbean islands \parencite{Jentes:2011et}. Surveillance of \gls{wt} \gls{yfv} is rarely done in any comprehensive way, however the \gls{wt} lineage is divided into 4 basal genotypes, East and West Afica, East and West South America \parencite{Bryant:2007kx}. Risk of \gls{yfv} infection is reasonably thought to associate with proximity to vector habitat, which requires both sufficient rain and low altitude \parencite{Jentes:2011et}.

\section{Pathogenesis and Tissue Tropism}
\Gls{yfv} infection begins from the bite of an infected hematophagous mosquito, typically \emph{Aedes spp.}. Then, the virus is thought to infect resident antigen-presenting cells in the skin, which transport the virus to a draining lymph node; flaviviruses are frequently reported to infect Langerhans and other \gls{dc} types \parencite{Johnston:2000cf, Querec:2006kr, Wu:2000ew}. From there, a primary viremia and febrile signs may develop between 3 and 6 days post-infection. After a resolution phase in which signs and symptoms decrease in severity, a minority of cases (12\%) may advance to a toxic phase, featuring liver damage and hemorrhage, for which the mortality rate has been recently estimated to be 47 percent \parencite{Johansson:2014hv}. Length of \gls{yf} disease course has been described for nonhuman primates, \parencite{Monath:1981tc}, however the most recent data on intrinsic incubation periods in humans (time from infection to first sign of fever) was inferred by a modeling study to be 4.6 days (95\%\gls{ci}=4.1-5.1) \parencite{Johansson:2010ca}. Secondary viremia targeting the liver is referred to as \emph{visceroptropic} disease. \Gls{yfv} is directly tropic to hepatocytes, which in histological sections reveals the presence of viral antigen, councilman bodies, and an ordered pattern of hepatocyte necroses and apoptosis in midzonal region of the infected acinus \parencite{Quaresma:2006hu,Quaresma:2006jb}. The destruction of regular liver parenchymal architecture and consequent alteration to hemoglobin recycling produces characteristic jaundice; the yellowed skin in advanced cases is the color from which the disease derives its name (Latin \emph{flavus}: ``yellow''). The \Gls{who}-recommended case definition for laboratory workup of suspected \gls{yf} disease includes only the observation of jaundice within two weeks of acute fever \parencite{WorldHealthOrganization:2003tq}. Tissue tropisms for \gls{yfv} have been extensively reviewed \parencite{Vasconcelos:2015bp}. 

\section{Early Development of YFV Vaccines}
Live-attenuated vaccines to prevent \gls{yf} were among the first to be successfully developed by empiric serial passage, and as such occupy a place of historical significance in the study and control of tropical diseases. The development pathway of early \gls{yfv} vaccines was notable for several technical advances that rendered the virus amenable to laboratory manipulation. The discovery that Indian crown (then identified as \emph{Macaca sinica}) and rhesus (\emph{Macaca mulatta}) macaques were susceptible to \gls{yfv} infection permitted the isolation of \gls{wt} strains in the absence of a cold chain \parencite{Stokes:1928uh}. Max Theiler first demonstrated that the virus could be propagated in the brains of mice, also showing that the phenotype of the virus would change with serial passage \parencite{Theiler:1930gy}. The susceptibility of mice to \gls{ic} challenge by \gls{yfv} was then usefully employed in serological protection assays, in which sera were titrated against lethality of a \gls{yfv} strain with neurotropic phenotype \parencite{Theiler:1931tp}. These methods were used to successfully isolate two \gls{wt} strains in 1927, these strains forming the basis of two concurrently developed, and then successfully deployed live-attenuated \gls{yfv} vaccines. The first of these \gls{wt} strains, called Asibi, was isolated from a Ghanaian of the same name, who suffered a mild case of YF and fully recovered \parencite{Stokes:1928uh}. The second was isolated from the Syrian Francois Mayali, which became known as the \gls{fvv}, and is referred to in literature of the time as the “French” strain, whether as \gls{wt} or in passaged, attenuated strain forms \parencite{Sellards:1928wb}.

\subsection{Development of the French Strain}
\Gls{fvv} was adapted mouse brain (formerly also called as “fixed”) by 128 passages by Theiler before transfer to the Institut Pasteur at Dakar, Senegal \parencite{Theiler:1930gy,Durieux:1956wx}. \Gls{wt} viscerotropic properties of the strain were attenuated, leading to the investigation of the strain as a human vaccine. Neurotropism in monkeys was enhanced, a property reasonably attributed to fixation of the virus to mouse brain \parencite{Lloyd:1933wv,Ni:2000un}. At attenuated passage levels, the vaccine strain is referred to as the “neurotropic” strain, and later the “\gls{fnv}”. \Gls{fnv} served as the platform by which to investigate \gls{yfv} vaccine delivery methods, including the co-administration with the virus of human immune serum \parencite{Sawyer:1932vb}. The attendant impracticality of serovaccination, in conjunction with both the limited supply of serum and risk of pathogen contamination precluded widespread adoption of this technique. Through the 1940’s onward, \gls{fnv} was used extensively in French west Africa, with 84 million doses produced between 1939 and 1954, which were administered by cutaneous scarification from an Arabic gum suspension of infected and desiccated mouse brain; \gls{fnv} was frequently co-administered with smallpox vaccine \parencite{Durieux:1956wx}. Reference materials for \gls{fnv} are exceedingly rare, however a number of \gls{fnv} collection strains were partially sequenced in reference to the parental strain \gls{fvv} and compared for neurotropic properties \emph{in vivo} \parencite{Wang:1995vu}.

Although \gls{fnv} was an excellent vaccine, there was recognition that it was responsible for unacceptable incidence of post-vaccinal encephalitis in children (approximately 3:1000)\parencite{STONES:1955uj}. Initially, this was deemed acceptable due to the greater risk to children in endemic areas of contracting \gls{yf}. As the vaccine gained success in the control of \gls{yf} disease in Francophone Africa, administration of \gls{fnv} to children under 10 years was prohibited in 1961 \parencite{Vainio:1998wy}. In 1980, \gls{fnv} was discontinued by the Institut Pasteur. 

\subsection{Deveplopment of the 17D Strain}
The Asibi strain was passaged 53 times in monkeys before attempting to attenuate by passage in non-native tissues. From this juncture in handling of the \gls{wt} strain, it was referred to as the “pantropic” or Asibi strain, meaning that the virus possessed both neurotropic and viscerotropic affinities. Under supervision of Max Theiler at the Rockefeller Foundation laboratories in New York, the Asibi strain was passaged in successive tissue preparations; a passage series called 17D was performed in minced mouse embryo (18x), minced whole chick embryo (58x) and finally minced whole chick embryo without brain and spinal cord (100x) \parencite{Theiler:1937wy}. At stages along the passage series, the virus was assayed for neurotropism in mice by \gls{ic} injection and pathogenicity in monkeys by both \gls{ic} and extraneural administration. At the 114th passage (in reference to the parental strain Asibi), the virus was observed to have lost pathogenicity for both mice and monkeys. For monkeys challenged by \gls{ic} or subcutaneous routes with the 114th passage of 17D, the strain produced a survivable encephalitis, with transient fever. 17D did not cause significant viremia in monkeys, and as such the virus was considered to have lost \gls{wt} viscerotropism. The 176th passage of the virus was less lethal by average survival time in mice than a comparator strain at the 114th passage, when delivered \gls{ic} and was selected for use as a vaccine. 17D infects \emph{Aedes spp.} mosquitoes, however the dose required is greater than that required of for \gls{wt} strains \parencite{Whitman:2015tu}. The attenuated strain does not disseminate to the tissues of the mosquito, preventing transmission \parencite{Miller:1988td}. The simultaneous loss of viscerotropism, neurotropism, and infectivity to mosquitoes ultimately rendered 17D to be safer than \gls{fnv}, although immunogenicity of 17D was lower than that of the French strain \parencite{Anonymous:1946tm, Stuart:1953uaa}. As with \gls{fnv}, field immunogenicity trials of 17D were first undertaken using co-administration of normal human serum with the virus (non-aqueous 17D). 17D preparations without added serum (aqueous-base) were standardized in 1945, and is the current form of the vaccine; administration is by the subcutaneous route \parencite{Quarantine:1945ui}. There have been few changes in the seed-lot system since 1945 and over 600 million doses of 17D have been distributed \parencite{Barrett:2009ed}.

\section{Efficacy and Use to the Public Health}
17D vaccines are considered an essential component of childhood vaccination schedules in \gls{yf} endemic countries, although the early 21st century has brought increased attention on programmatic lapses in \gls{yfv} vaccination as a matter of economic development. Though surveillance of \gls{yfv} is incomplete across the endemic range, recent estimates of \gls{yf} disease burden modeled from African serosurveys and case data indicate that 1.3 million cases (95\%\gls{ci} = 850,000-1.8 million) would have occurred in 2013, resulting in 78,000 deaths (95\%\gls{ci} = 19,000-180,000)  \parencite{Garske:2014dp}. Vaccination coverage was estimated to have prevented 450,000 cases (95\%\gls{ci} = 340,000-560,000) and 28,000 deaths (95\%\gls{ci} = 7,200-62,000) in the same year, attesting \emph{in silico} to a quantified, reduced burden of disease as a consequence of YF vaccination campaigns. Widespread mass vaccination campaigns in a number of African countries with \gls{fnv} and 17D vaccines between the 1940s and 1960s had resulted in the almost-complete disappearance of yellow fever. However, immunization campaigns waned in the mid-1960s. Consequently, since the mid-2000s the nongovernmental \gls{gavi} has committed considerable resources to support of childhood \gls{yfv} vaccination in endemic countries, including efforts supporting increased production of 17D vaccines from \gls{who}-prequalified manufacturers \parencite{GAVI:C4b7wMHq}. Since 2006, 12 countries have completed preventive yellow fever vaccination campaigns involving over 70 million doses of vaccine with financial support from \gls{gavi} Alliance, and its partners including the \gls{echo} and the \gls{cerf}. These programs are ongoing, including a major program of vaccination in Nigeria. 

Currently, over 35 of the 44 countries at risk for yellow fever in Africa and the Americas have routine infant immunization programs. Large scale vaccination campaigns require enormous amount of vaccine. To meet increasing demand for vaccine, \gls{id} delivery of 17D has been investigated as an alternative to subcutaneous administration under a hypothesis that less virus would be needed to induce protection; the strategy represents both a dose-saving approach to vaccination and a potential administration route for recipients with egg allergy (the vaccine is produced in embryonated chicken eggs). In healthy adult recipients, immunogenicity and safety measurements were equivalent in a comparison of \gls{id} delivery (1/5 typical dose) of 17D to the same lot of vaccine administered conventionally \parencite{Roukens:2008dc}. Additionally, a retrospective cohort (n=7) of individuals who failed the 17D skin allergy test (\gls{id} administration of 1/5 typical dose) were found to have seroconverted subsequent to the test \parencite{Roukens:2009fu}. Results are promising, however more studies are needed to ascertain the true dose-response relationship of \gls{id} vaccine load with adverse events, immunogenicity, and viremia.

\section{Lineage of 17D and the Current Vaccine Standard Genotype}
The lineage of 17D has been extensively described, consisting of seed and vaccine lots from the three “substrains” 17D-204, 17D-213, and 17DD \parencite{WorldHealthOrganization:2010wo,Monath:2013cu}, where \emph{seed lot} is a regulatory term used to define a particular batch of vaccine strain virus that is rigorously qualified for both phenotype and genotype before use in the production of bulk vaccine batches \parencite{WorldHealthOrganization:2010wo}. The aforementioned technical standards define the use of \emph{primary} and \emph{secondary} seed lots, which define a single-passage chain to arrive at the vaccine, e.g. secondary seed is a single egg passage from the primary seed, and the vaccine is a single passage from the secondary seed, respectively. By means of the seed-lot system, the exact passage level of vaccine production batches is known. 

Briefly, Theiler’s originating 17D strain at passage 176 was taken and the basal substrains 17D-204 and 17DD originate from subculture levels 204 and 195, respectively [Figure \ref{YFV_current}]. The 17D-213 substrain was produced by the \gls{rki} in 1977 as a derivative of 17D-204, to be free from contamination by \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{alv}}\gls{alv}, a retrovirus producing sarcomas in chickens, but with no known pathogenic effects in humans \parencite{Bres:1987wl}. All 17D seeds in current use are free of \gls{alv}, and standard production methods require use of eggs from \gls{alv}-free flocks \parencite{WorldHealthOrganization:2010wo,Monath:2013cu}. Since the 1940s there have been many vaccine producers from over 10 countries but currently there are only six producers: United States (17D-204, YF-Vax\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, Sanofi-Pasteur), France (17D-204, Stamaril\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, Sanofi-Pasteur), Senegal (17D-204, Institut Pasteur, Dakar), the People’s Republic of China (17D-204, Tiantan\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, Wuhan Institute of Biological Products), the Russian Federation (17D-213, Chumakov Institute of Poliomyelitis and Viral Encephalitides), and Brazil (17DD, Bio-Manguinhos/\gls{fiocruz}). The French, Russian, Senegalese and Brazilian producers are prequalified by the \gls{who}, serve international markets, and are used for mass vaccination campaigns. The U.S. and Chinese vaccines are used in domestic markets only. 

Initial genomic sequence comparison of 17D in reference to the \gls{wt} parental Asibi strain was performed using a 17D-204 isolate obtained from the \gls{atcc} \parencite{Hahn:1987tw}. Similar information was later reported for the 17DD and 17D-213 substrains of the vaccine, which permitted elucidation of the conserved sequence features between all substrains \parencite{dosSantos:1995wq}. Current data indicates that the three vaccine substrains share 20 common amino acid substitutions, and four nucleotide substitutions in the 3’\gls{utr} [Table \ref{WHO_standard_YFV}]. The contribution of the various amino acid substitutions and 3’\gls{utr} nucleotide changes to the attenuated phenotype is poorly understood (see below). Minimal consensus sequence divergence has been noted between substrains of 17D. Bayesian phylogenetic analysis for a set of 17D substrain consensus sequences was unable to resolve taxonomic groupings for 17D-204 and 17D-213 isolates, indicating very limited sequence divergence between these substrains for the clustering model employed \parencite{Stock:2012gp}. These data are consistent with the very effective seed-lot system developed in the 1940s and probably contribute to the excellent safety record of 17D vaccine.

Little is known about the molecular basis of attenuation for the 17D vaccine. The 17D and \gls{fnv} vaccines share two common attenuated mutations in reference to their parental strains (\gls{m}-L36F and \gls{ns}4B-I95M), however the influence of these residues on attenuation is unknown. The residue \gls{m}-36 (L in Asibi, F in 17D) is contained in a pro-apoptotic sequence described for dengue virus \parencite{Catteau:2003dl}. Using a series of 17D/Asibi point mutants localized in the envelope protein, Lee and colleagues observed that the capacity of 17D to bind to heparin sulfate was reduced for viruses containing the \gls{wt} residues E-380T and E-325P (the latter only found in 17D-204 and 17D-213 substrain vaccines); the substitutions also produced a low-neurovirulence phenotype in mice \parencite{Lee:2008gd}. In summary, determinants of attenuation have been explored to a limited extent. Findings thus far have indicated the influence of alterations in binding of virus to cells, but the effects are likely multigenic. The \gls{wt} and vaccine strain may differ in capacity to antagonize innate immune responses in the host, although this has not been definitively shown for any 17D genotype \parencite{MunozJordan:2005ia}.

Genetic determinants of mosquito infectivity for the vaccine were investigated by Higgs and coworkers, in which Asibi-17D chimeric infectious clones were constructed to contain swapped residues in \gls{ns}2A (four amino acids), \gls{ns}4B (one amino acid), and the 3’\gls{utr} (four nucleotides) \parencite{McElroy:2006wb}. Dissemination of the chimeric viruses to salivary glands of orally infected \emph{Aedes aegypti} was reduced for \gls{wt} (Asibi) backbone constructs bearing 17D residues. In \emph{Ae. aegypti} the 17D envelope residue E-380R was paradoxically observed to increase titers of the Asibi strain in mosquito salivary glands \parencite{Huang:2014cy}. Although not explaining attenuation in the vaccinee, reduced mosquito infectivity of 17D is hypothesized to contribute to the safety profile of the vaccine by preventing transmission of the attenuated virus post-administration.

\section{Nonclinical Comparison of 17D Substrains}
Nonclinical comparison of 17D substrain vaccines is infrequently reported. Vaccine seeds are evaluated using a standard WHO monkey neurovirulence assay, in which 10 rhesus or cynomolgous (\emph{Macaca fascicularis}) macaques are administered a known quantity of seed virus \gls{ic}, and monitored for 30 days. Clinical and histological outcomes of neurotropism are compared to an equivalently sized group challenged with a reference strain of known acceptable properties; viscerotropism (serum viremia) and immunogenicity (\gls{prnt50}) are assessed by numeric limits. Minor and coworkers used this method to compare three 17D seeds of differing substrain origin \parencite{Minor:2011hk}. The \gls{who} reference virus strain 168-73 (17D-213 substrain) was of equivalent immunogenicity to secondary seeds originating from Senegal and the United Kingdom (both 17D-204), however greater viremias and lower histological scores were observed for 168-73 when compared to the seeds. 

\section{Clinical Comparison of 17D Substrains}
Very little clinical information exists on the comparative performance of vaccines derived from the 17D substrains in human subjects, as modern efficacy trials would be considered unethical due to known historical properties of the vaccine and the severity of \gls{yf} disease. Some non-inferiority trials have been reported for 17D-derived products, both within and between substrains. A comparison of two 17D-204 substrain products Stamaril\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and Arilvax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} (Chiron; originally produced by Wellcome labs in the U.K. and no longer manufactured) was performed in a healthy adult cohort (n=211) \parencite{Lang:1999wi}. Both vaccines were found to be immunogenic, observing similar rates of local and systemic adverse reactions; the test group receiving Stamaril\textsuperscript{\textregistered} developed moderately but significantly higher neutralizing antibody titers at days 10 and 28 post-administration. Again for two products within the 17D-204 substrain, Arilvax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and YF-Vax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} were compared in a cohort of Peruvian children of ages ranging from 9 to 10 years, observing similar rates of adverse events for both products; seroconversion rates differed significantly and were 94.9\% and 90.6\%, respectively \parencite{BelmustoWorn:2005tt}.  

For studies comparing 17D vaccines produced from different substrains, a series of trials were reported by \gls{fiocruz} comparing the immunogenicity and reactogenicity of two serially generated Brazilian 17DD substrain products with that of a 17D-213 substrain derivative vaccine \parencite{Camacho:2004ui,Camacho:2005vb}.  In a healthy adult cohort (n=1087), neutralizing antibody titers were equivalent for the 17DD vaccines investigated, while moderately higher for the vaccine derived from the 17D-213 substrain, although immunogenicity results for all groups would be considered highly protective. In seronegative recipients, seroconversion rates for the three test groups were equivalent ($\geq$98\%), and no significant differences in rates of post-vaccinal adverse events were reported. Comparison of the 17D-204 substrain Stamaril\textsuperscript{\textregistered} with 17D-213 substrain products RKI-YF\textsuperscript{\textregistered} (\gls{rki}, Germany) and Berna-YF(Flavimun\textsuperscript{\textregistered}) (neither vaccine produced currently) was reported in a cohort of healthy adults (n=304), from which was observed statistical dissimilarity of neutralizing titers between the groups receiving Berna-YF\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and Stamaril\textsuperscript{\textregistered} \parencite{Pfister:2005tm}. Statistically significant difference of neutralizing titers was not observed between groups receiving RKI-YF\textsuperscript{\textregistered} and Berna-YF\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, and all subjects seroconverted (\gls{prnt50}$\geq$1:10); the result is of particular interest for the reason that the two vaccines are identically related on the 17D lineage, arising from passage 228 relative to the parental strain and from the same secondary seed. For a cohort of healthy Argentinian adults and children (n=2514, ages=1-70 years), the \gls{fiocruz} 17DD vaccine was analyzed for comparative safety to a vaccine derived from 17D-213 substrain; no statistical differences in either local or systemic adverse events were reported, immunogenicity data were not analyzed \parencite{Ripoll:2014fz}. In summary, there is little evidence to suggest divergence of phenotype across the 17D substrain lineage. Differences of immunogenicity observed in clinical trials exist at very high ranges of neutralizing antibody titer, and as such would not likely represent any meaningful change in protection. Trials comparing outcomes for 17D vaccines are summarized in Table \ref{substrain_comparison_Trials}.


\section{Use of 17D as a Recombinant Backbone of Other Vaccines}
Owing to the considerable safety profile of 17D, the nonstructural genes of the vaccine have been used as a backbone in a number of vaccine candidates designed to deliver both flaviviral and non-related antigens. Specifically, infectious clones of 17D virus have been generated with the 17D genome \gls{cdna} in one or more plasmids; using reverse genetics the \gls{cdna} can be mutated to swap or insert foreign sequences. The \gls{cdna} is transcribed to \gls{rna} \emph{in vitro} (which is equivalent to \gls{yfv} genomes), transfected into cells, and is able to replicate in cells to make a recombinant virus carrying desired mutations \parencite{Rice:1989vo}. Thus, the 17D genome is variably amenable to insertion of heterologous antigenic elements; the strategy is used in a number of flavivirus vaccine candidates at various preclinical and clinical development stages.  Recombinant 17D virus bearing inserts of known \emph{Plasmodium} epitopes showed moderate immunogenicity to the insert peptides in monkeys, while both immunogenicity to the 17D backbone and viremia were reduced in comparison to the parental 17D vaccine, which was hypothesized to result from partially abrogated endosomal fusion in the recombinant virus \parencite{Bonaldo:2005ht}. A recombinant 17DD construct expressing from the \gls{asp2} of \emph{Trypanasoma cruzi} was partiality protective in a susceptible mouse model, a significant result considering the dearth of preventatives and treatments for Chagas’ disease \parencite{Nogueira:2013kj}. A recombinant 17D was investigated as a backbone to stimulate cellular immunity against \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{hiv}}\gls{hiv} by delivering a known CD8$^+$ T cell epitopes \parencite{Bonaldo:2010ey}. The epitope was derived from a known immunodominant section of the \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{siv}}\gls{siv} Gag polyprotein, ligated to be expressed by a nonstructural region of the 17D genome. With this construct, CD8$^+$ T cell responses to the 17D vector were stimulated in a nonhuman primate model, with specific cellular immunogenicity to the \gls{siv}/gag polyprotein. Recombinant 17D virus with \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{lav}}\gls{lav} glycoproteins were generated by insertion of the \gls{lav} GP1 and GP2 genes into the C-terminal region of 17D E protein gene; the construct was immunogenic in a guinea pig model of \gls{lav} infection, although less protective (80\%) by comparison to a live-attenuated Mopeia virus/\gls{lav} reassortant \parencite{Bredenbeek:2006fq}.

The ChimeriVax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} (Sanofi-Pasteur, Lyon, France) platform is perhaps the most advanced in development of this class of vaccines, and is derived by swap chimeras of heterologous flavivirus prM/M-E structural cassettes into the nonstructural backbone of the 17D genome \parencite{Bray:1991ti}. Resulting recombinant vaccines are efficiently delivered in a live-attenuated context with a safety profile afforded by the 17D nonstructural genes \parencite{Guy:2011cq}. To date, one chimera, ChimeriVax-JE (Imojev\textsuperscript{\textregistered}), has progressed to licensure and is registered in nine countries \parencite{Appaiahgari:2010kr}. The most advanced candidate in development using this technology is the tetravalent \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{denv}}\gls{denv} preparation. Two phase III, multi-center trials of the ChimeriVax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} \gls{denv} candidate were recently reported, testing efficacy to prevent hospitalized dengue disease in Asian and Latin American pediatric cohorts, for which combined-serotype efficacy was 56.5\% and 60.8\%, respectively \parencite{Capeding:2014fj,Villar:2015jpa}. Similarly, the ChimeriVax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{wnv}}\gls{wnv} candidate WNV02 was recently assessed in a dose-ranging, phase II safety trial enrolling 208 healthy adults for three age cohorts, revealing 96 percent seroconversion at 28 days that was correlated with vaccine dose \parencite{Monath:2006ft, Biedenbender:2011ic}. While these studies with mosquito-borne flaviviruses suggest that the nonstructural protein genes of 17D encode the attenuated phenotype, utilization of the ChimeriVax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} technology for a tick-borne encephalitis virus prM/M-E chimera did not produce the desired attenuated phenotype in preclinical studies \parencite{Rumyantsev:2013cl}.

\section{New Report of A Serious Adverse Reaction to 17D}
\Glspl{sae} following vaccination with 17D are rare, and generally are categorized as either neurotropic, referring to infection of the central nervous system (\gls{yeland}) or viscerotropic (\gls{yelavd}), referring to multi organ disease with infection of the liver as is similarly observed in \gls{wt} \gls{yf}. Clinical data on \gls{sae} are consequently rare, but support a paradigm of \gls{sae} occurrence that is considerably influenced by host factors rather than reversion of the vaccine virus to a virulent phenotype.
	
A recent case of fatal \gls{yelavd}, the 65th reported case of this type, occurred in 2014, in a female recipient receiving the vaccine for travel to South America \parencite{DeSilva:2015ww}. The patient developed signs consistent with the \gls{who} case definition of \gls{yelavd}, eventually succumbing to systemic disease \parencite{Gershman:2012ji}. \Gls{yfv} antigen was observed in recovered tissues by immunostain, with positive IgM titer of 1:640. On autopsy, a thymoma was found in association with serology indicating myasthenia gravis, which if previously identified would potentially have contraindicated receipt of the vaccine. The directly precedent reported cases of \gls{yelavd} occurred in 2009, which was not reported in open literature \parencite[952]{Monath:2013cu}. Preceding this in 2007, in Peru, five individuals were treated for systemic viscerotropic disease following 17D vaccination in a large campaign, with four fatalities \parencite{Whittembury:2009hy}. The consensus genomic sequence of the originating vaccine lot and three of the fatal cases were determined, and there was no sequence divergence from the secondary seed lot used in manufacture. Single-nucleotide polymorphism content from the 2007 cluster and previous \gls{yelavd} case tissues were sequenced and compared at loci hypothesized to influence innate immune responses, but the results did not describe association of particular \glspl{snp} with the cases. Because the case descriptions include evidence of altered immune status for the vaccinees in association with viscerotropic disease, host factors are likely contributors.
	
Post-vaccinal \Gls{sae} have been extensively reviewed \parencite{Barrett:2009ed}. Risk factors for \gls{yelavd} are estimated from the limited clinical data available and include age ($>$60 years), systemic lupus erythematosus, thymectomy/thymoma, or other autoimmune condition \parencite{Monath:2005cw, Staples:2010ei}. A hypothesis that competent innate immune responses are required to prevent dissemination of 17D is supported by animal models of \gls{yfv} disease, in which 17D vaccine virus is lethal in mice lacking $\alpha$/$\beta$/$\gamma$ \gls{ifn} receptors (AG129) \parencite{Meier:2009ij}. Similarly, widespread dissemination of genetically tagged 17D strains was observed in a mouse model lacking $\alpha$/$\beta$ \gls{ifn} receptors \parencite{Erickson:2013kv}. In a study comparing naive 17D recipients in young and elderly age groups, post-administration viremias in the older subjects were found to be relatively greater \parencite{Roukens:2011dm}. Mutations localized to CCR5/RANTES genes or their regulatory elements are associated with variable response to viral infection, and thus have been hypothesized to influence development of \gls{yelavd}, however the association of known alleles with the adverse syndrome is inconclusive \parencite{Pulendran:2008eu}.

\section{New Vaccine Candidate Technologies and Monitoring of Safety}
As aforementioned, the occurrence of adverse events is rare, however the use of inactivated \gls{yfv} vaccines has been proposed to ameliorate concerns of adverse reactions for recipients for whom administration of live 17D strains would be contraindicated, including the elderly, immunosuppressed, or those with egg allergy. Design of inactivated \gls{yfv} vaccines have typically been frustrated by necessity to deliver multiple doses to achieve protection in typical neurovirulent mouse models of \gls{yfv} infection.
	
Nonclinical data for two inactivated, whole-virion 17D candidates has been reported. Characterization of the $\beta$-propriolactone-inactivated 17D-204 candidate XRX-001 (Xcellerex/GE Healthcare, Marlborough, MA) was performed, showing that the vaccine was protective in Syrian golden hamsters against challenge by the hamster-adapted Jimenez strain of \gls{yfv} \parencite{Monath:2010bu}. Immunogenicity of XRX-001 was assessed in cynomolgous macaques for two and three-dose administration schedules, from which sera variably neutralized the heterologous 14-FA (Angola) strain. Results of a phase I safety and dose-ranging trial of XRX-001 in 60 recipients were reported in 2011, observing seroconversion (endpoint/baseline \gls{prnt50}$>$4) for all participants receiving a booster dose at 21 days \parencite{Monath:2011kx}. Although neutralizing antibody titers for the two-dose administration of XRX-001 did not exceed those typically found in clinical studies of live-attenuated 17D vaccines, the seropositivity criterion used (\gls{prnt50}$>$10) is thought to confer protection in humans \parencite{Camacho:2004ui}. Two inactivated 17D candidates have been reported by the Oswaldo Cruz Foundation (Brazil). The first of these to be characterized was a pressure-inactivated, 17DD-substrain candidate; immunogenicity data in mice revealed that three doses were necessary to produce equivalent neutralization titers to those subjects receiving live 17DD vaccine \parencite{Gaspar:2008hj}. The second, a $\beta$-propriolactone-inactivated 17DD substrain derivative was tested for immunogenicity in outbred mice (subcutaneous route), using a live-attenuated 17DD vaccine as comparison. Greatest immunogenicity (\gls{gmt}=922, 95\%\gls{ci} = 666-1274, 43.75\% seroconversion) was observed in a group receiving three doses, adjuvanted with aluminum hydroxide \parencite{Pereira:2015kk}. No information is available on the durability of protection offered by any inactivated 17D vaccine, however under reported test conditions, immunogenicities for these exceed typical standards for seroconversion, and as such would be expected to be protective.
	
Recombinant \gls{yfv} vaccine candidates have been investigated using diverse vectoring strategies. Very commonly, constructs are designed to deliver the 17D prM/M-E structural cassette, which contains the predominant surface-exposed neutralizing epitopes of \gls{yfv} and all flaviviruses. Delivery of \gls{yfv} antigens by live-attenuated, recombinant vaccinia constructs was shown to be feasible in the late 1990s \parencite{Pincus:1992ta}. A recent effort used the replication-deficient \gls{mva} and \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{dvv}}\gls{dvv} to vector the 17D prM/M-E antigen protected mice from \gls{ic} challenge with 17D when administered in a single dose (17D substrains, though highly attenuated in humans, are virulent for mice when directly introduced to the brain) \parencite{Schafer:2011hy}. The study not only demonstrated protection and immunogenicity of the constructs, but also high apparent safety in mice, as the vaccines themselves were not neurotropic when administered at doses exceeding those required to induce protection. A plasmid-vectored \gls{dna} vaccine was constructed by fusion-ligation of the 17DD substrain prM/M-E onto that of human \gls{lamp1}, a strategy designed to target the antigen structural subunit for efficient trafficking to MHC-II compartments and consequent presentation and stimulation of cellular immune responses \parencite{Maciel:2015dc}. A 45 day, three-dose course of the \gls{lamp1} plasmid construct was protective when assayed in mice against live 17DD virus delivered \gls{ic}.


\section{New Insights on Durability of Protection for 17D}
Production of \gls{yfv} vaccines is regulated using a seed-lot system, developed in 1945 in response to evidence that phenotypic instability of the vaccine could be introduced by improper handling resulting in excessive passaging of the vaccine virus \parencite{Quarantine:1945ui}. Among other contributing findings, an early Brazilian trial cohort observed absence of immunogenicity for a group receiving vaccine from a seed at extended passage levels \parencite{Soper:1938tb}. Presently, the protection conferred by 17D production seed lots is characterized for immunogenicity by neutralization antibody titers during performance of the \gls{who} monkey neurovirulence test (criterion: \gls{prnt50} $>$1:10), and by comparison trials in humans for lots of vaccine originating from newly generated seeds \parencite{WorldHealthOrganization:2010wo}. Presently, a neutralization titer of 1 in 10 or a fourfold increase of neutralizing antibody from baseline is considered evidence of seroconversion. \Gls{ihr} require that, for certain travelers in areas of \gls{yfv} endemicity, a certificate of prophylaxis (“yellow card”) be issued by the vaccinator, which is considered valid for ten years following the tenth day post-administration \parencite{Assembly:2006uc}.

The \gls{who} \gls{sage} working group for \gls{yf} vaccination evaluated open and closed-literature findings on the durability of immunogenicity conferred by 17D \parencite{Anonymous:6Th9TjOR}. Specifically, the working group recommended revision to the 2003 \gls{who} position on 17D booster schedules, recommending that both the \gls{who} position and \gls{ihr} be revised to reflect an expectation that 17D confers lifetime protection without the necessity of 10-year booster intervals. The amendment to the \gls{ihr} was approved by the World Health Assembly, to be included in a 2016 revision. In February 2015, the \gls{acip} working group on yellow fever vaccines presented these findings to the \gls{acip}, which were adopted by unanimous vote of the committee \parencite{NCIRD:2015wm}. The 2013 \gls{sage} findings were assembled into an extensive review of immunogenicity for 17D, which in numerous cases of seropositivity have been observed in vaccinees at intervals greater than 10 years post-administration \parencite{Gotuzzo:2013cg}. Briefly, of 8 open-literature studies considered by the working group, duration of 17D immunity was maintained for 10 years in all datasets, with an upper reported boundary of 38 years \parencite{Niedrig:1999wo}. Some countries have questioned the proposed removal of booster doses and it remains to seen whether or not all will elect to adopt the \gls{who} recommendation.

\section{New Insights on Immunogenicity for 17D}
Mechanisms underlying the long-lasting immunogenicity of 17D are poorly understood, but are attributed in part to the combined stimulation of innate, humoral and cellular immunities from the presentation of endogenous antigen expressed by the live, replicating virus \parencite{Mudd:2010fm}. In a passive serum transfer experiment using the hamster adapted challenge model, neutralizing \gls{prnt50} titers of $<$1:40 were sufficient to confer protection, supporting the canonical model for protective humoral seroconversion in humans \parencite{Julander:2011hq}. Systems biology approaches have recently been deployed to interrogate these mechanisms, in order to more closely elucidate determinants of immunogenicity for the vaccine in the host. Significantly, studies of this type have identified key cytokines and innate response elements that determine both the diversity and longevity of the immune response to 17D. A series of \emph{in silico} studies reported associations of networked antiviral response elements that were predictive of CD8$^+$ T cell responses to the vaccine \parencite{Querec:2008jj}. In a comparative study of several vaccines, humoral responses to 17D were partially correlated to cellular stress responses, specifically that of transcription factor ATF2, and translation factor kinase EIF2AK4, both markers for stress response and amino-acid starvation, which contributes to programming of \glspl{dc} to present antigens to stimulate CD8$^+$ T cell immunity \parencite{Li:2014he}. The cellular response to 17D is highly diverse, involving activation of multiple \gls{tlr} response elements and lineages of \glspl{dc} \parencite{Gaucher:2008hm}. This results in the induction of various pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IL-12p40, IL-6, and interferon-$\alpha$, and consequently the stimulation of both T and B cell responses. In a cohort of healthy adults receiving 17D vaccine, the viral load at day 14 post-administration correlated with effector CD8$^+$ T cell responses; a saturation effect for CD8$^+$ T cell response is observed above a certain viremia sustained in the vaccinee, above which no changes were observed \parencite{Akondy:2015ks}. The study is especially significant to understanding the immunological responses of 17D recipients under conditions of dose-sparing. In a comparison of 17DD and 17D-213 substrains in a pediatric cohort, the cytokine responses of the vaccines were similar, however revealing an enhanced inflammatory component for the group receiving the 17D-213 substrain vaccine \parencite{CampiAzevedo:2012gt}. \Gls{ifn}-gamma responses are observed to correlate with humoral, CD4$^+$ T cell, and CD8$^+$ T cell responses \parencite{Neves:2010fa, Neves:2013ce}. It is hoped that, by interrogation of specific immune response elements, predictive models of response to live-attenuated vaccines could be constructed, and in such a manner offer personalized insight to the safety and eventual protection available to the recipient.


\section{New Paradigms and Legacy Technologies}
As aforementioned, 17D is manufactured in embryonated chicken eggs, a legacy technology that remains unchanged since standardization of the vaccine in the 1940s. The adaptation of the vaccine to other cell based systems has been investigated, with the goal of efficiently producing the vaccine \emph{in vitro}. The adaptive capacity of \gls{yfv} has been investigated since the early development of all \gls{yfv} vaccines, with multiple studies recognizing that passage history could reverse the attenuation or immunogenicity of the vaccine \parencite{FINDLAY:1935vu,Soper:1938tb}. A 17DD substrain seed was adapted to \glspl{cef}, to permit growth of the vaccine lot by microcarrier bead culture technology \parencite{Freire:2005iw}. The \gls{cef}-prepared vaccine lot was tested using the standard \gls{who} monkey neurovirulence test; the authors noted elevated neurotropism scores in the monkeys tested with respect to a conventionally prepared seed, although these results were not statistically significant. Adaptation of the inactivated 17D-204 candidate XRX-001 to Vero cell culture was performed, revealing some instability in the vaccine genome localized to the envelope, \gls{ns}2A, and \gls{ns}4B genes, although differences in phenotype conferred by mutations have not been tested in an infectious clone system \parencite{Beasley:2013ff}. The substitutions were stably observed between three independently conducted passage series, associated with greater yields of the virus from Vero cell cultivation.  In both of these cases, the vaccines were safe and immunogenic in nonclinical studies involving nonhuman primates, however the known potential for instability of live virus phenotype under \emph{in vitro} passage adaptation warrants close attention.

To this end, massively parallel sequencing (\gls{ngs}) approaches have been deployed to interrogate the fine population structure of vaccines, including the presence of contaminants \parencite{Neverov:2010ko}. \index{Quasispecies}These methods partially derive from theoretical insight of the \emph{quasispecies} paradigm, in which viral diversity is considered to influence fitness \emph{in vivo} \parencite{Andino:2015bg}. Recently, a commercial lot of YF-Vax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} 17D-204 vaccine (Sanofi-Pasteur, Swiftwater, USA) was sequenced by massively parallel methods recovering evidence of very low \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{alv}}\gls{alv} contamination, confirmed by \gls{rtpcr}  \parencite{Victoria:2010kf}. Low coverages for \gls{yfv} (n$<$100) prevented complete assessment of population structure for the targeted viruses. Comparison of a 17D-204 commercial lot with a collection lot of the parental Asibi strain was performed at ultra deep coverage ($>$5000), revealing that the \gls{wt} virus population structure differs from that of the vaccine by virtue of relative homogeneity suggesting that the lack of diversity in the 17D vaccine virus population may contribute to the attenuated phenotype (study is presented in chapter 3)  \parencite{Beck:2014cm}. Resolution of population structure for viral vaccines by \gls{ngs} or other methods is a potential avenue by which to measure the presence of virulent subpopulations which naturally occur in \gls{rna} viruses, and in doing so predict safety profiles of the vaccines before use.

\subsection{Quasispecies Theory and Fitness of RNA Viruses}
The complexity of \gls{rna} virus populations, although speculated by observations of intermediate phenotype on passage, was first rigorously shown in a set of passage experiments using the \gls{rna} bacteriophage Q$\beta$ \parencite{Domingo:1978wg}. For a set of specific variant populations identified through T1 oligonucleotide fingerprints, assays were performed to competitively passage the variants in equivalent multiplicity to that of a diverse \gls{wt} phage, observing that the \gls{wt} phage was more fit in \emph{E.coli} with respect to growth rate. Additionally in this study, clonally selected populations of the virus were homogenous when subjected to T1 oligonucleotide fingerprinting, indicative of a bottleneck effect. \index{Quasispecies!And viral fitness}In a later review, it was proposed that the 1978 findings of Domingo et. al. had observed an effect in which rare variants were negatively selected, and by that means had achieved only low equilibrium frequency in the \gls{wt} competition assay \parencite{Holland:1982kp}. Likewise, a population of minority variants arising in \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{fmdv}}\gls{fmdv} were serially passaged and observed to be unstable \parencite{Sobrino:1983wn}; the authors interpreted the results as a confirmation of the findings of Domingo et. al. (1978), only in the context of a virus causing mammalian disease \parencite{Domingo:1978wg}. 

The apparent cloud of variably fit genomes is formally referred to as a \emph{mutant spectrum}, from which the particular dynamics of the population are measured. Within the spectrum are considered to exist \emph{minority variants}, \index{Quasispecies!Causal Mechanisms}which may arise randomly by biophysical mechanisms; the generation, timescale, and persistence of these variants in the viral population is considered a possible signal of not only the selection pressures encountered by the virus population, but also specific determinants of the fitness of the population as a whole. For \gls{rna} viruses specifically, population complexity is considered to arise from a combination of large population size, short generation times, and a lack of error-proofing or correction in genome replication. These effects have been extensively reviewed \parencite{Andino:2015bg, Domingo:2012hw}.

Several possible mechanisms are observed by which viral fitness is influenced by diversity of the population. First, the presence of random, discrete minority variants may confer increased fitness to the particles carrying the subpopulation allele. Second, the viral population may itself be a unit of selection; this is a formal quasispecies effect. An effect of this type was demonstrated by Vignuzzi et. al, in which a high-fidelity polymerase mutant of \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{pov}}\gls{pov} was shown to be of low fitness to produce neurotropic disease in a mouse model; chemical mutagenesis rescued the effect in the absence of the capacity of the virus to mount a typical, \gls{wt} population structure \parencite{Vignuzzi:2006ju}. These results were interpreted as a \emph{cooperativity} effect, in which the change of fitness was attributed to the viral population \emph{en masse}. 

\subsection{Viral Diversity and Pathogenesis}
In addition to early studies using \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{fmdv}}\gls{fmdv}, numerous studies attest to the pathogenic influence of viral minority variants; these effects may be diverse, differentially influencing tissue tropism or immune responses. In many cases, studies are designed to reconstruct changes in viral diversity under selective pressure of passage or treatment. It is likely that pharmaceutical treatment of some viral infections is effective in part due to a population-level effect. In human samples, the population complexity of \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{hbv}}\gls{hbv} was reduced in response to entecavir treatment, while high viral diversity was associated with advanced liver fibrosis \parencite{Nishijima:2012ew}. Likewise, in a group of transplant patients, diversity of \gls{hcv} was associated with an immunocompromised state \parencite{Pessoa:1999gl}. The study of both \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{hcv}}\gls{hcv} and \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{hiv}}\gls{hiv}-1 quasispecies effects admit the role of ``founder'' and ``transmitter'' genotypes, which are thought to be minority variant populations with sufficient tropism to surmount an initial bottleneck when productively infecting a new host \parencite{Stoddard:2015bg}. In a study in which mice lacking $\alpha$/$\beta$-interferon receptors were challenged with \gls{yfv} 17D, the subjects contracted a disseminated infection, suggestive that some low level of diversity in the attenuated vaccine is insufficient to traverse the innate immune response. For \gls{yfv} this is substantiated by infrequent occurrence of \gls{yelavd}, which in some cases is associated with administration of 17D to persons with history of immunocompromise \parencite{Whittembury:2009hy,DeSilva:2015ww}.


\subsection{Viral Diversity and Ecology of Arboviruses}
Resolution of population structure effects for arthropod-borne viruses like \gls{yfv} is complicated by the complexity of the \gls{wt} transmission cycle, which for \gls{yfv} may include, at minimum, productive infection of (1) a short-lived mosquito vector and a (2) a mammalian host, likely nonhuman primate, which may or may not result in a dead-end infection. Although quasispecies effects have not been rigorously described for arboviruses, one prominent framework is the ``trade-off'' hypothesis, which indicates that a certain level of diversity propels infection across barriers to which the virus is poorly adapted; e.g. viral fitness is partially compromised in all hosts, if the virus must infect them all to propagate. This phenomenon is shown by cell culture passage studies, in which adaptation of the virus to one passage host compromises fitness, while alternating passage in multiple, representative  culture systems does not \parencite{Coffey:2008jqa}. 

Mechanistic origins of viral diversity through the full transmission cycle are poorly understood; some evidence has been reported to suggest that for mosquito-borne viruses, alternating patterns of diversification (in one host) with purifying selection and (in the mammalian host) is typical; this was shown for \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{wnv}}\gls{wnv} in alternating passage of chicks and \emph{Culex pipiens} mosquitoes \parencite{Deardorff:2011bd}. The phenomenon was observed as a loss of fitness to infect mice for strains of \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{rvfv}}\gls{rvfv} that were passaged exclusively in either Aag (\emph{Aedes aegyptii}) or BHK21 cells; this implies that for \gls{rvfv}, the multiple host transmission cycle maintains the stability of the genome \parencite{Moutailler:2011hh}. Local tissue effects are likely to govern the ecologies of specific transmission cycles; a study in which \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{veev}}\gls{veev} was passaged in mosquitoes revealed that stepwise infection events in the body of the insect represent population bottlenecks for the virus, increasing homogeneity of the population \parencite{Forrester:2012da}. For \gls{yfv}, the dynamics of population complexity with respect to the virus ecology are not known, but it is likely that some level of high diversity exists in \gls{wt} strains due the numerous host and vector types that are infected in nature \parencite{Ellis:2008jt}.

\subsection{Deep Sequencing and Technical Approaches}
The dynamics and content of subpopulations for \gls{yfv} are unknown, but could influence the phenotype and safety of vaccine products; the resolution of vaccine population structure represents a novel class of methods to ensure the stability of attenuating genotypes for vaccine strains. Recently, the use of deep sequencing techniques has been deployed to assay viral diversity directly from nucleic acids isolated from preparations of a veterinary \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{rabv}}\gls{rabv} vaccine, with subsequent measurements of distance applied to assess divergence \parencite{Hoper:2015fs}.  Deep sequencing of vaccine has been extensively reviewed \parencite{Luciani:2012em,Kennedy:2011ff,Neverov:2010ko}. For \gls{yfv}, the ideal use case for \gls{ngs} would be in the qualification of vaccine lots once filled. More essentially in cases of regeneration for primary or secondary seed lots, \gls{ngs} would ideally be used to ensure negligible divergence of the vaccine population genotype from one previously accepted or qualified. Significantly, \gls{ngs} methods would facilitate the development of a predictive model by which to assess the phenotype conferred by subpopulation alleles in the vaccine under consideration.

\subsection{Connecting Viral Diversity to Flavivirus Vaccinology}
For \gls{yfv} specifically, some evidence has been put forth to suggest that the virus is genetically stable as a general phenomenon; e.g. \gls{yfv} strains are observed to incorporate very few mutations per generation. This property is also inferred phylogenetically; a study comparing consensus sequence divergence over time for \index{Viruses, Miscellaneous!\Acrfull{denv}}\gls{denv} and \gls{yfv} revealed that, over the timescale of the phylogeny, \gls{yfv} sequences mutated at a significantly lower substitution rate per unit time \parencite{Sall:2010db}. It is likely, however that this result occurred due to the differing ecologies of \gls{yfv} and \gls{denv}; \gls{yfv}, though occasionally emergent, is consistently maintained in nature and is not known to be influenced by expansion dynamics, whereas the four \gls{denv} serotypes are in geographic flux \parencite{Messina:2014jo, Afreen:2015hj}. Therefore, the reported phylogenetic stability of \gls{yfv} is more likely a consequence of the limited host range to which the virus had previously expanded and adapted. 

Studies on the 17D vaccine have shown the strain to be of high stability, with a very limited amount of sequence diversity arising across passage. In a study of the 17D strain, in this case the Arilvax\textsuperscript{\textregistered} 17D-204 substrain vaccine product, Sanger sequencing revealed a set of nucleotide positions in the vaccine with noncoding heterogeneity, as evidenced by divided chromatogram populations \parencite{Pugachev:2002vu}. In particular, the mutation at E-A240V, a site previously attributed to influence neurovirulence of the vaccine strain \parencite{Ryman:1997fh}. A later study by the same laboratory passaged the 17D vaccine under controlled conditions with subsequent conventional sequencing; the mutation rate of 17D was estimated by this study to be $1.9 \times 10^{-7}$ - $2.3 \times 10^{-7}$ substitutions per replicated \gls{rna} molecule \parencite{Pugachev:2003fj}. The aforementioned study of 17D strains by Bayesian phylogenetic methods found poor evidence for diversification of the vaccine along the length of the vaccine lineage; there was notably poor resolution of known branch points in the lineage, indicating either an equilibrium of selective pressures along the genotype or very strong purifying selection of the vaccine genotype in egg culture \parencite{Stock:2012gp}. Owing to the uniquely stable features of 17D, the population structure of the virus is likely to contain key signals of attenuation.

\section{Specific Aims}
The \textbf{overall objective} of the dissertation research is to characterize typical population structures for \gls{yfv} vaccine strains, and in doing so provide context for the influence of population-level effects on the attenuation mechanisms of 17D and other live-attenuated viral vaccines. The \textbf{central hypothesis} of the dissertation research is that population structures of \gls{wt} \gls{yfv} strains are distinct from those of the vaccine strain 17D. This hypothesis was tested by the analysis \emph{in silico} of the population structures for \gls{yfv} 17D vaccine strains of known phenotype, with the ultimate goal of resolving elements of \gls{yfv} quasispecies effects that may influence the phenotype of the vaccine. The overall hypothesis for Aim 1 is that by using deep sequencing to compare a commercial lot of 17D vaccine with the parental strain from which the vaccine was derived, evidence of the tissue selection process will be resolved, most evidently by changes in nucleotide diversity. The overall hypothesis for Aim 2A is that by comparing a set of standardized and phenotypically stable 17D vaccine seed strains, evidence will be provided for the stability of the vaccines on a subpopulation level; these results will provide a framework for evaluating small changes in population structure for the vaccines over long passage histories represented by the seeds. Also as a question of passage stability, the overall hypothesis for Aim 2B is that in a comparison of quasispecies structure between the prototype \gls{wt} strain Asibi and a hamster-adapted (hamster/P7) strain of the same, specific viral subpopulations influencing viscerotropy in the hamster model will be observed. The overall hypothesis for Aim 3 is that by using a deep sequencing study to compare the population structures of collection strains of \gls{fnv}, it will be found that previously described differences in neurotropism measures for the strains will be accompanied by destabilization of the strain populations. The overall hypothesis for Aim 4 is that when using deep sequencing to compare the population structures of the \gls{wt} \gls{jev} SA14 strain to the vaccine derivative SA14-14-2, patterns of diversity change recovered in the original Asibi/17D comparison will similarly be observed.

\subsection{Specific Aim 1}
\textbf{Aim:} Compare population structure of the \gls{yfv} parental strain Asibi with that of the vaccine derivative 17D-204.

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Quasispecies structures of \gls{yfv} strains Asibi and 17D-204 strains of \gls{yfv} are distinct, and informative for studies of vaccine attenuation.

\textbf{Rationale:} There is previous evidence by classical techniques that \gls{yfv} has some level of population diversity, but this is completely undescribed for \gls{yfv}.

\subsection{Specific Aim 2A}
\textbf{Aim:} Analysis by deep sequencing of \gls{yfv} vaccine strains and seeds from a historical collection representing the entire lineage of the 17D vaccine substrains.

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Population structures of standardized 17D production seeds will show evidence of stability, with considerable similarity of nucleotide content between strains.

\textbf{Rationale:} It is not known as to whether the subpopulations of \gls{yfv} vaccine seeds are stable in relation to each other.

\subsection{Specific Aim 2B}
\textbf{Aim:} Analyze changes in population structure for a visceroptropic \gls{yfv} strain produced by serial passage adaptation of the Asibi strain in hamster liver.

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Population structures of the \gls{wt} Asibi strain and that of the Hamster/P7 strain are distinct, and will particularly identify changes in diversity during serial passage.

\textbf{Rationale:} Due to other evidence that passage, this dataset is of particular importance to understanding the population dynamics involved in the adaptation of \gls{yfv} to cause severe liver disease.

\subsection{Specific Aim 3} 
\textbf{Aim:} Translate findings of population structure for 17D to characterize the French neurotropic virus, a discontinued and unsafe \gls{yfv} vaccine.

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Instability of population structure is associated with instability of neurovirulent phenotype for a set of \gls{fnv} collection strains.

\textbf{Rationale:} Since \gls{fnv} strains were previously characterized to be unequally neurotropic, \emph{in silico} analyses will reveal instability of consensus identity, diversity, nucleotide selection, and single-nucleotide variation.

\subsection{Specific Aim 4} 
\textbf{Aim:} Extend analyses of \gls{yfv} strains by resolving population structure of \gls{jev} strain SA-14-14-2, an empiric flaviviral vaccine.

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Patterns observed \emph{in silico} for \gls{yfv} vaccines will be recapitulated for a comparison of the \gls{wt} \gls{jev} strain SA-14 and the live-attenuated vaccine strain SA-14-14-2.

\textbf{Rationale:} Previous work observed that the \gls{yfv} vaccine 17D-204 was of homogenous population structure relative to the \gls{wt} parental strain Asibi. As SA14-14-2 was attenuated by serial passage, this pattern will be similarly observed in the \gls{jev} dataset.
